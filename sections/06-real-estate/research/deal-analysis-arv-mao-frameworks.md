## ULTRATHINK Section: Deep Reasoning on Deal Analysis

### Why Do Most Investors Overestimate ARV?

**Cognitive Biases at Play:**
1. **Optimism Bias:** Investors emotionally attached to a deal see best-case scenarios
2. **Confirmation Bias:** Cherry-picking comps that support desired ARV rather than objective analysis
3. **Recency Bias:** Using recent peak prices in declining markets
4. **Anchoring:** Letting the seller's asking price influence perceived value

**Market Conditions That Invalidate the 70% Rule:**
- **Hot Markets (2020-2022 conditions):** With 20%+ annual appreciation, the 70% rule was too conservative—investors could succeed at 80-85%
- **Cooling Markets (2023-2024 conditions):** With flat or declining prices, 70% becomes dangerous—may need 65% or lower
- **High-Interest Rate Environments:** Holding costs spike, requiring more margin
- **Low Inventory Markets:** Competition drives acquisition costs up, compressing margins
- **Rural vs. Urban:** Rural markets have thinner buyer pools, requiring larger margins for safety

**How Toby Analyzes Deals Differently Than Textbooks:**
1. **Conservative ARV:** Always uses the LOWER end of comp range, not middle
2. **Real Holding Costs:** Includes "surprises" buffer (10-15% of rehab)
3. **Market Timing Awareness:** Adjusts rule percentage based on current market velocity
4. **Exit Strategy Flexibility:** Analyzes deals with multiple exit strategies simultaneously
5. **Relationship Leverage:** Factors in contractor relationships that reduce costs

**The $130k Louisiana Property Analysis (Toby's ASCEND Coaching Example):**
- **Situation:** Student found property at $130k, ARV appeared to be $180k
- **Thin Margin Problem:** $180k × 70% = $126k MAO, minus rehab left almost nothing
- **Toby's Guidance:** "When margins are this thin, you're not investing—you're gambling. The deal needs to work at 65% rule, or you walk."
- **Lesson:** Thin-margin deals require either (a) significantly lower acquisition price, (b) value-add strategies not reflected in comps, or (c) passing on the deal

### 1. After Repair Value (ARV) Calculation Methodology

**Definition:** ARV is the estimated market value of a property AFTER all renovations are complete. It represents what a retail buyer would pay for the finished product.

#### Step-by-Step ARV Calculation Process

**Step 2: Identify Comparable Sales (Comps)**

**The 5 Rules of Comp Selection:**
1. **Location:** Same neighborhood or comparable area (within 1 mile ideal, 3 miles max)
2. **Recency:** Sold within 90 days (prefer 30-60 days in volatile markets)
3. **Similarity:** Within 200 sq ft and same bed/bath count (±1)

### 2. The 70% Rule Framework

**The Formula:**
```
Maximum Allowable Offer (MAO) = (ARV × 70%) - Repair Costs
```

**Example Calculation:**
```
ARV: $250,000
70% of ARV: $175,000
Estimated Repairs: $40,000
MAO: $175,000 - $40,000 = $135,000
```

#### Why 70%? Breaking Down the Numbers

| Category | Percentage | Purpose |
|----------|------------|---------|
| Purchase Price | ~60-65% | Acquisition cost |
| Repair Costs | ~15-20% | Renovation expenses |
| Holding Costs | ~5-8% | Interest, taxes, insurance, utilities |
| Selling Costs | ~8-10% | Agent commissions, closing costs |
| Profit | ~10-15% | Investor return |
| **Total** | **~100%** | |

The 30% "spread" covers:
- Holding costs during renovation
- Selling costs (commissions, closing)
- Profit margin
- Buffer for surprises

#### When the 70% Rule DOES Apply

- Standard fix-and-flip projects
- Normal market conditions (balanced supply/demand)
- Moderate rehab scope ($20k-$60k)
- 3-6 month project timeline
- Conventional financing or cash purchase

#### When the 70% Rule DOES NOT Apply

**Use 65% or Lower When:**
- Heavy rehab required ($80k+)
- Structural issues present
- Market is cooling/declining
- Extended timeline expected (6+ months)
- High interest rate environment (carrying costs spike)
- First deal (experience buffer needed)

**Can Consider 75-80% When:**
- Very hot, appreciating market
- Light cosmetic rehab only
- Strong buyer demand (quick sale expected)
- Exceptional financing terms (low/no interest)
- Experienced investor with contractor relationships
- Multiple exit strategies available

**Toby's Modified Rule for Current Markets (2024-2025):**
```
Conservative MAO = (ARV × 65%) - Repair Costs - (ARV × 5% surprise buffer)
```

### 5. Common ARV Mistakes and How to Avoid Them

#### Mistake #1: Using Active Listings as Comps
**The Error:** Including properties currently listed (not sold) in ARV calculation
**Why It's Wrong:** List prices are aspirational; only sold prices represent market value
**The Fix:** Use ONLY closed sales; pending sales can be secondary reference only

#### Mistake #2: Cherry-Picking High Comps
**The Error:** Selecting only the highest-priced comps to justify desired ARV
**Why It's Wrong:** Creates unrealistic expectations and thin margins
**The Fix:** Use ALL relevant comps; let the data tell the story

#### Mistake #3: Ignoring Days on Market (DOM)
**The Error:** Using comp that sold quickly vs. one that sat for 6 months without adjustment
**Why It's Wrong:** Long DOM often indicates overpricing or issues
**The Fix:** Factor DOM into analysis; properties with 60+ DOM may indicate ceiling prices

#### Mistake #4: Overlooking Condition Differences
**The Error:** Comparing subject's planned renovation to comps with different finish levels
**Why It's Wrong:** A "flip-grade" renovation differs from "builder-grade" or "custom"
**The Fix:** Physically view or virtually tour comps when possible; verify finish levels

#### Mistake #5: Not Accounting for Market Shifts
**The Error:** Using 90-day old comps in a rapidly changing market
**Why It's Wrong:** Markets can shift 5-10% in 90 days during volatile periods
**The Fix:** Apply time adjustments; prioritize most recent sales; monitor market trends

#### Mistake #6: Single-Comp Reliance
**The Error:** Basing ARV on just one comparable sale
**Why It's Wrong:** One data point is insufficient; could be anomaly
**The Fix:** Always use minimum 3 comps; 5-6 provides stronger confidence

#### Mistake #7: Emotional Attachment
**The Error:** Falling in love with property and forcing numbers to work
**Why It's Wrong:** Emotions cloud judgment; bad deals feel good until they don't
**The Fix:** Have a neutral third party review analysis; trust the numbers

## Toby's ASCEND Deal Coaching Examples

### Case Study 1: The Louisiana $130k Thin-Margin Deal

**Student Situation:**
- Found property listed at $130,000
- Believed ARV was $180,000
- Estimated $30,000 in repairs
- Asked Toby: "Should I proceed?"

**Toby's Analysis:**
```
ARV: $180,000
70% Rule: $180,000 × 70% = $126,000
MAO: $126,000 - $30,000 = $96,000

Student's proposed price: $130,000
Gap from MAO: $34,000 OVER maximum

At $130k purchase:
Sale Price: $180,000
- Purchase: $130,000
- Repairs: $30,000
- Holding (6 mo): ~$8,000
- Selling costs: ~$15,000
= LOSS: ($3,000)
```

**Toby's Coaching:**
"This isn't a deal—it's a job you're paying to work. When margins are this thin, one surprise (foundation issue, extended holding period, market shift) turns a break-even into a disaster. The deal needs to work at 65% or you walk. In this case, that means $87,000 max purchase. If seller won't accept $90k-$95k, move on. There are plenty of deals."

**Lesson:** Never force thin-margin deals. The spread exists to protect you from unknowns.

### Case Study 2: The Texas Market Competition Deal

**Student Situation:**
- Hot Texas market (2023)
- Property with multiple offers
- ARV: $320,000
- Repairs: $45,000
- Competing at 75% rule to win bid

**Toby's Analysis:**
```
Standard 70% MAO: ($320,000 × 70%) - $45,000 = $179,000
Student's aggressive offer: ($320,000 × 75%) - $45,000 = $195,000

Profit comparison at $195k purchase:
ARV: $320,000
- Purchase: $195,000
- Repairs: $45,000
- Holding: $12,000
- Selling: $22,000
= Profit: $46,000 (14.4% of ARV)
```

**Toby's Coaching:**
"In competitive markets, you CAN adjust the rule, but only when: (1) You have verified the ARV with strong comps, (2) Your contractor relationship gives you confidence in repair estimate, (3) The market velocity supports quick sale, (4) You have fallback exit strategy (rental numbers work). At $195k, you're still at 14% gross—acceptable for experienced investors. But if you're newer, stay at 70% and lose more deals. Better to miss deals than lose money."

**Lesson:** Adjust rules based on experience and market conditions, not desperation.

### Case Study 3: The Foundation Question

**Student Situation:**
- Great price on property with known foundation issues
- ARV: $200,000
- Asking: $85,000
- Foundation repair estimate: "Around $15,000"

**Toby's Analysis:**
```
On paper at 70%:
MAO: ($200,000 × 70%) - repairs = $140,000 - repairs
At $85k asking, massive spread available

BUT: Foundation work reality check
- "Around $15k" often becomes $25-40k
- Pier-and-beam vs. slab foundations are DIFFERENT animals
- Slab repairs are more expensive and less predictable
- Foundation issues often reveal plumbing issues underneath
```

**Toby's Coaching:**
"Foundation deals are where rookies get destroyed. That $15k estimate? Get three quotes. If it's a slab foundation, add 50% contingency minimum. And ALWAYS get a plumbing scope—foundation cracks often mean broken pipes. The deal might still work at $85k, but your repair number should probably be $50-60k all-in, not $30k. Build the numbers conservatively. I've seen 'great deals' with foundation issues turn into money pits because investors didn't understand what they were buying."

**Lesson:** Foundation issues require extra due diligence and larger contingencies.

## Recommended Exercises

### Exercise 1: Comp Analysis Deep Dive
**Purpose:** Master the skill of identifying and adjusting comparable sales

**Method:**
1. Select a property currently listed for sale in your target market
2. Identify 5 comparable sales from the last 90 days
3. Create adjustment worksheets for each comp
4. Calculate three ARV scenarios: conservative, middle, aggressive
5. Compare your ARV to the listing price—is it priced correctly?
6. Document your reasoning for each adjustment

**Time Required:** 2-3 hours
**Repeat:** Weekly until comp analysis feels natural

---

### Exercise 2: The 70% Rule Stress Test
**Purpose:** Understand when the 70% rule applies and when to adjust

**Method:**
1. Find 3 deals that were successfully flipped in your market (use investor networks)
2. Reverse-engineer the numbers: What % did they actually purchase at?
3. Calculate what would have happened at 65%, 70%, 75%
4. Interview the investor if possible: What made them comfortable with their number?
5. Document lessons learned about local market rule application

**Time Required:** 4-5 hours including research
**Repeat:** Monthly to stay calibrated to market

---

### Exercise 3: The Conservative ARV Challenge
**Purpose:** Train yourself to default to conservative analysis

**Method:**
1. Analyze a deal using your normal process
2. Now re-analyze using ONLY the lowest comp
3. Calculate MAO using 65% rule instead of 70%
4. Add 15% contingency to repair estimate
5. Does the deal still work with ultra-conservative numbers?
6. If yes, this is a STRONG deal. If no, proceed with caution.

**Time Required:** 1 hour per deal
**Apply:** To every deal before making offers

---

### Exercise 4: Market Velocity Assessment
**Purpose:** Determine appropriate rule percentage for current market conditions

**Method:**
1. Pull data for your target neighborhood:
   - Average days on market (DOM) last 30/60/90 days
   - Months of inventory
   - List-to-sale price ratio
   - Number of active vs. sold listings
2. Score your market:
   - DOM <30 & <2 months inventory = Hot (can use 75%)
   - DOM 30-60 & 2-4 months = Normal (use 70%)
   - DOM >60 & >4 months = Cooling (use 65% or lower)
3. Adjust your standard rule accordingly
4. Reassess monthly

**Time Required:** 1-2 hours monthly
**Tool:** Create spreadsheet to track over time

---

### Exercise 5: The Deal Autopsy
**Purpose:** Learn from deals that didn't work out (yours or others')

**Method:**
1. Find a flip deal that lost money or barely broke even
2. Reconstruct the original analysis:
   - What was the projected ARV?
   - What were estimated repairs?
   - What rule % was used?
3. Identify where analysis went wrong:
   - ARV overestimated?
   - Repairs underestimated?
   - Market shifted?
   - Holding period extended?
4. Document the specific lessons
5. Create a "mistake checklist" to prevent repeating

**Time Required:** 2-3 hours per case study
**Repeat:** Analyze at least 3 failed deals before your first purchase

## Key Takeaways

1. **ARV Accuracy Is Everything:** A 10% overestimate can eliminate your entire profit margin
2. **The 70% Rule Is a Starting Point:** Adjust based on market conditions, experience level, and deal complexity
3. **Conservative Analysis Wins Long-Term:** Aggressive investors make money sometimes; conservative investors build wealth consistently
4. **Comps Must Be Relevant:** Location, recency, and condition matter more than finding high prices
5. **Trust the Numbers, Not Emotions:** If the math doesn't work, no amount of wanting will make it profitable
6. **Build Contingencies:** Surprises are guaranteed; budget for them
7. **Practice Before Playing:** Analyze 50+ deals on paper before risking real money
