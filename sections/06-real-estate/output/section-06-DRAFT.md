## INTRODUCTION: THE DEAL THAT TAUGHT ME EVERYTHING

A student brought me a deal last week. Single-family house in Louisiana. Purchase price: $85,000. Estimated ARV: $130,000. Rehab estimate: $25,000.

On paper? Looks good. Let me show you what he saw:

```
ARV: $130,000
70% Rule: $130,000 × 0.70 = $91,000
Maximum Offer: $91,000 - $25,000 rehab = $66,000
His Price: $85,000

"It's close, Toby. I just need to negotiate a little."
```

But here's what he MISSED.

**Problem #1: His ARV Was Wrong**

He used the highest comp he could find—a completely renovated house that sold for $142,000. He averaged it with one other sale at $118,000 and called it $130,000.

I pulled up the actual data. Five comparable sales in the last 90 days:
- $118,000
- $122,000
- $125,000
- $128,000
- $142,000 (the outlier—custom finishes, corner lot, pool)

Conservative ARV? Not $130,000. It's $122,000. The $142k sale was an anomaly. You don't price your deal on anomalies.

**Problem #2: His Rehab Estimate Was Fantasy**

$25,000 for a property with:
- Foundation concerns (pier-and-beam, visible sagging)
- Roof that was "probably okay for a few more years"
- Original electrical panel from 1972
- Galvanized plumbing throughout

I asked him: "Did a contractor walk this property?"

"Not yet. I estimated from photos."

Photos don't show what's hiding under the house. Photos don't reveal a $15,000 foundation repair. Photos don't catch the electrical that won't pass inspection.

My estimate after a quick review: $40,000-45,000 minimum. Add 20% contingency for a property this old: $48,000-54,000.

**Problem #3: He Had No Exit Strategy**

"I'll flip it."

"What if the market softens?"

"Then I'll hold it as a rental."

"What's the rent? What's your mortgage payment? Does it cashflow?"

Silence.

He hadn't run the rental numbers. If this deal went sideways, he had no backup plan.

**The Real Numbers:**

```
Conservative ARV: $122,000
70% Rule: $122,000 × 0.70 = $85,400
Realistic Rehab: $50,000
Maximum Offer: $85,400 - $50,000 = $35,400

His Asking Price: $85,000
Over Maximum By: $49,600
```

This wasn't a deal. It was a disaster waiting to happen.

I told him to WALK AWAY.

"But Toby, it pencils out on paper!"

"On YOUR paper, with your optimistic numbers. In reality? You'll lose $30,000 minimum. Trust me."

Three months later: Property still on market. Price dropped to $70,000. Previous buyer's financing fell through after inspection revealed foundation issues worse than expected.

**The Lesson:**

Conservative analysis protects you. Better to pass on 100 marginal deals than do ONE bad deal.

A bad deal doesn't just cost you money—it costs you TIME. Time you could spend finding good deals. Time you could spend building your empire. Time you can never get back.

## PART 1: DEAL ANALYSIS FUNDAMENTALS

Every successful real estate investor lives and dies by their deal analysis. Get this wrong, and nothing else matters. Get this right, and you build wealth.

### The 70% Rule (And When It Applies)

The 70% Rule is the foundational formula for evaluating fix-and-flip deals:

**Formula:**
```
Maximum Allowable Offer (MAO) = (ARV × 70%) - Repair Costs
```

**Example Calculation:**
```
ARV: $200,000
70% of ARV: $140,000
Estimated Repairs: $40,000
Maximum Allowable Offer: $140,000 - $40,000 = $100,000
```

**Why 70%?**

The 30% "spread" accounts for:
- **Holding costs (5-8%):** Mortgage payments, insurance, utilities, property taxes while renovating
- **Selling costs (8-10%):** Agent commissions (5-6%), closing costs (2-3%), staging, marketing
- **Profit margin (10-15%):** Your reward for risk, time, and capital deployment
- **Surprise buffer (2-5%):** Because something ALWAYS goes wrong

[VISUAL MARKER: 70% Rule Breakdown Diagram - Pie chart showing cost allocation]

**When the 70% Rule APPLIES:**
- Standard fix-and-flip projects
- Normal market conditions (balanced supply/demand)
- Moderate rehab scope ($20k-$60k)
- 3-6 month project timeline
- Conventional financing or cash purchase

**When the 70% Rule DOES NOT Apply:**

**Use 65% or Lower When:**
- Heavy rehab required ($80k+)
- Structural issues present (foundation, roof)
- Market is cooling/declining
- Extended timeline expected (6+ months)
- High interest rate environment (carrying costs spike)
- This is your FIRST deal (experience buffer needed)

**Can Consider 75-80% When:**
- Very hot, appreciating market
- Light cosmetic rehab only
- Strong buyer demand (quick sale expected)
- Exceptional financing terms (low/no interest)
- Experienced investor with contractor relationships
- Multiple exit strategies available

[CALLOUT BOX: TOBY'S TOUGH LOVE #1]
> "The 70% Rule is a STARTING point, not a guarantee. In 2024-2025, with higher interest rates and uncertain markets, I'm coaching students to use 65%. Would you rather lose deals to conservative analysis or lose MONEY to aggressive analysis? I know my answer."

### Calculating After Repair Value (ARV) Correctly

ARV is the estimated market value of a property AFTER all renovations are complete. It represents what a retail buyer would pay for the finished product.

**The Most Common ARV Mistake:**

Using the highest comp as your ARV.

Rookies look at comparable sales and think, "That house sold for $180,000—mine will too!" No. That house might have been priced perfectly, staged professionally, and sold to an emotional buyer in a bidding war. You're not getting that price.

**The Correct Method:**

**Step 1: Find 3-5 SOLD Comps**
- Not listed. Not pending. SOLD.
- Closed in the last 90 days (prefer 60 days in volatile markets)
- Within 0.5 miles (1 mile max in rural areas)
- Similar square footage (within 20%)
- Similar bed/bath count (±1)
- Similar condition AFTER your renovation

**Step 2: Make Adjustments**

No two properties are identical. Adjust for differences:

| Feature | Typical Adjustment |
|---------|-------------------|
| Square Footage | $30-$100/sq ft (market dependent) |
| Bedroom | +/- $5,000-$15,000 each |
| Bathroom | +/- $5,000-$12,000 each |
| Garage | +/- $10,000-$25,000 |
| Pool | +/- $10,000-$30,000 (can be negative in some markets) |
| Lot Size | $1-$5/sq ft |
| Age/Condition | $5,000-$50,000 for major differences |

**Step 3: Calculate Conservative ARV**

Toby's Method (use this):
- Take the LOWEST adjusted comp as your primary indicator
- Weight recent sales more heavily
- Default to lower end in uncertain markets

Standard Method:
- Average all adjusted comp values
- Remove outliers (highest and lowest if using 5+ comps)
- Round to nearest $5,000

[CALLOUT BOX: TOBY'S TOUGH LOVE #2]
> "Most rookies overestimate ARV by 20%. They look at the highest comp and say 'That'll be mine!' No. You're not getting top dollar. You're not a professional stager. You don't have the perfect buyer waiting. Price conservatively or get burned. The deal that looks good with aggressive numbers doesn't exist—only the deal that looks good with conservative numbers is real."

### Maximum Allowable Offer (MAO) Calculation

Here's the step-by-step process:

**Step 1:** Calculate ARV (conservative average of comps)
**Step 2:** Estimate rehab costs (we'll cover this in Part 2)
**Step 3:** Decide your strategy and percentage:

| Strategy | Formula | Notes |
|----------|---------|-------|
| Wholesale | MAO = (ARV × 0.65) - Rehab | Need margin for end buyer |
| Fix-and-Flip | MAO = (ARV × 0.70) - Rehab | Standard flip formula |
| Fix-and-Flip (Conservative) | MAO = (ARV × 0.65) - Rehab | First-timers, uncertain markets |
| Buy-and-Hold (Rental) | Different formula | Cashflow-based, covered below |

**For Buy-and-Hold Properties (Cashflow Analysis):**

The 70% Rule doesn't apply the same way. Instead, work backward from cashflow:

```
Monthly Rent: $1,500
Operating Expenses (50% rule): $750
Net Operating Income: $750

Target Cash-on-Cash Return: 10%
Annual NOI: $9,000
Maximum Cash Investment: $90,000

If putting 25% down: Maximum Purchase Price = $90,000 / 0.25 = $360,000
But verify: Does the mortgage payment + expenses stay under rent?
```

The key question for rentals: **Does it cashflow from day one?**

If the answer is no, or "it will once rents go up," or "appreciation will make up for it"—walk away. Negative cashflow properties are not investments. They're liabilities.

### Why Rehab Estimates Are Always Wrong (And How to Fix It)

[CALLOUT BOX: TOBY'S TOUGH LOVE #3]
> "Your $20K rehab will become $40K. Always. Budget overruns are the norm, not the exception. I've coached hundreds of investors, and I can count on one hand the ones who came in under their original estimate. Add 50% contingency or prepare to lose money."

**Why Estimates Fail:**

1. **Hidden Issues:** Foundation cracks, electrical problems, plumbing failures, termite damage, mold—all hiding behind walls and under floors
2. **Scope Creep:** "While we're at it, let's upgrade the..." adds up fast
3. **Contractor Errors:** Missed items in scope, underestimated complexity, subcontractor issues
4. **Material Cost Increases:** Prices can change 10-20% between estimate and purchase
5. **Extended Timeline:** More time = more holding costs = more overrun

**The Cascade Effect:**

```
Initial Estimate: $30,000
+ Foundation issues discovered: +$12,000
+ Plumbing needs replacing (found during foundation work): +$6,000
+ Electrical not to code (inspector flagged): +$5,000
+ Extended timeline (6 extra weeks): +$4,500
+ Contractor markup for "emergency" additions: +$2,500
= Actual Cost: $60,000
```

Your $30,000 just became $60,000. If you didn't budget for this, your profit is gone.

**The Solution: The Contingency Rule**

| Rehab Level | Description | Base Contingency |
|-------------|-------------|------------------|
| Level 1 - Cosmetic | Paint, flooring, fixtures | 10% |
| Level 2 - Light | Cosmetic + kitchen/bath refresh | 15% |
| Level 3 - Moderate | Full kitchen/bath, some systems | 20% |
| Level 4 - Heavy | Systems, structural, major work | 25% |
| Level 5 - Gut | Down to studs, full rebuild | 30% |

Whatever your rehab estimate, add the appropriate contingency. If you come in under budget, great—that's extra profit. If you hit budget, you're covered.

### Toby's Foundation Work Insights

"Foundation work is where deals die. I've seen more investors lose money on foundation issues than any other single item. Here's what you need to know:"

**Pier-and-Beam Foundations:**
- **Advantage:** You can get under the house and SEE problems
- **Advantage:** Repairs are usually more straightforward
- **Common Issues:** Sagging beams, deteriorated piers, moisture damage
- **Typical Costs:** $3,000-$10,000 for pier replacement; $10,000-$25,000 for major repair
- **Toby's Rule:** "Always get under the house before closing. A $300 crawlspace inspection can save you $20,000."

**Slab Foundations:**
- **Challenge:** You CAN'T see what's happening underneath
- **Challenge:** Repairs require tunneling or breaking concrete
- **Common Issues:** Cracking, settling, plumbing failures under slab
- **Typical Costs:** $8,000-$30,000+ for pier installation; $5,000-$15,000 for plumbing tunnel
- **Toby's Rule:** "Always get a structural ENGINEER, not just a foundation company. Foundation companies will always find work to do—they make money fixing things. An engineer gives you an objective opinion. And ALWAYS camera-scope the plumbing. Slab cracks often mean broken pipes."

### Contractor Management

Remember Section 4 (Sales)? Those negotiation skills apply here too.

**Toby's Contractor Rules:**

**Rule 1: Fixed Bid, Not Hourly**
- Get a total project price, not hourly estimates
- Bid price is final—overages come from contractor's margin
- Protects you from "scope creep" charges

**Rule 2: Get 3 Bids Minimum**
- Compare apples to apples (same scope)
- Lowest bidder often cuts corners or underestimates
- Look for middle-range with best references

**Rule 3: Check References—Actually Call Them**
- Ask: "Did they finish on time? On budget? Any issues?"
- Visit a past job site if possible
- Ask if they'd hire them again

**Rule 4: Detailed Scope of Work**
- Written document, both parties sign
- Every item specified—nothing assumed
- "Paint all interior walls" vs. "Paint walls" (ceilings? Trim? How many coats?)

**Rule 5: Payment Schedule Tied to Milestones**
```
10% at signing (maximum)
30% at rough completion (demo, framing, rough MEP)
30% at finish start (drywall complete, flooring installed)
20% at substantial completion
10% holdback for 30 days (punch list protection)
```
Never pay ahead of completed work.

**Rule 6: Inspect Before Final Payment**
- Walk property with punch list
- Document every incomplete item
- Don't release final payment until complete

## PART 3: FINANCING STRATEGIES

The right financing can make a marginal deal profitable. The wrong financing can kill a great deal.

### The Four Financing Options (Cheapest to Fastest)

[VISUAL MARKER: Financing Options Comparison Table]

**Option 1: Conventional Financing**
- **Interest Rate:** 7-8.5% (investment property)
- **Down Payment:** 20-25%
- **Timeline:** 30-45 days
- **Best For:** Buy-and-hold rentals, strong credit borrowers

**Pros:**
- Lowest long-term cost
- Fixed rate stability
- Longest terms (30 years)

**Cons:**
- Slow closing
- Extensive documentation
- Property must be in good condition
- Credit and DTI sensitive

---

**Option 2: Hard Money**
- **Interest Rate:** 10-15%
- **Points:** 2-4 points upfront
- **Down Payment:** 10-20%
- **Timeline:** 7-14 days
- **Best For:** Fix-and-flip, competitive markets

**Pros:**
- FAST closing
- Asset-based (less credit scrutiny)
- Funds rehab costs

**Cons:**
- Expensive
- Short term (6-18 months)
- Points add up quickly

---

**Option 3: Private Money**
- **Interest Rate:** 8-12% (negotiable)
- **Terms:** Flexible
- **Timeline:** 3-14 days
- **Best For:** Any strategy, relationship-based

**Pros:**
- Negotiable terms
- Relationship-driven
- Can be very fast
- May fund 100% of deal

**Cons:**
- Requires network
- Takes time to build relationships
- Trust is essential

---

**Option 4: Creative Financing**
- **Seller Financing:** Owner carries note
- **Subject-To:** Take over existing mortgage
- **Lease Options:** Rent-to-own structure
- **Best For:** Motivated sellers, low-cash situations

**Pros:**
- Low or no money down possible
- Flexible terms
- Below-market rates possible

**Cons:**
- Requires motivated seller
- Complex structures
- Legal risks if not done properly

[CALLOUT BOX: TOBY'S TOUGH LOVE #4]
> "Hard money at 12% interest sounds expensive. You know what's more expensive? Missing the deal because you were trying to get conventional financing that takes 45 days. Speed costs money. Decide what matters. In a competitive market, the investor who can close in 10 days beats the one who needs 45 days every single time. Factor financing costs into your deal analysis—if the deal still works with hard money, DO the deal."

## PART 4: MARKET ANALYSIS & DEAL SOURCING

You can master deal analysis and still fail—if you're in the wrong market.

### How to Identify Good Markets (Before They're "Hot")

**Leading Indicators (Look for These):**
- Job growth (new companies moving in)
- Population growth (people moving TO the city)
- New construction (developers investing)
- Infrastructure investment (new roads, schools, transit)
- Days on market decreasing (inventory tightening)
- Rent growth outpacing price growth

**Lagging Indicators (You're Too Late):**
- Media calling it "hottest market!"
- Prices already up 30%+
- Bidding wars on every property
- Investors waiving inspections to compete
- Gurus promoting the market in courses

[CALLOUT BOX: TOBY'S TOUGH LOVE #5]
> "Chasing 'hot markets' is how you lose money. By the time CNBC is talking about a market, you're 12 months too late. The money was made by investors who identified the market BEFORE it was hot. How do you find those markets? You do the work. You study population data, employment trends, building permits. You talk to people on the ground. There are no shortcuts."

---

### The Sherman-Denison Lesson

"I coached a student who was excited about Sherman-Denison, Texas. On paper, it looked great:
- Low entry prices ($80-120k properties)
- Decent rent-to-value ratios
- Northern DFW spillover expected

Here's what the data didn't show:
- Limited economic diversity (dependent on few employers)
- Thin buyer pool (few retail buyers for flips)
- High investor competition (everyone saw the same 'opportunity')
- Slow absorption (properties sitting 90+ days)

The student bought a property. Renovated it beautifully. Listed at a fair price.

Four months later? Still on market. Had to reduce price twice. Eventually sold to another investor at minimal profit.

**The Lesson:** Analyze WHO is buying, not just WHAT is selling. If your exit is selling to other investors, you're competing for the same limited pool of buyers—and they all want deals too."

---

### Deal Sourcing Strategies

**Source 1: MLS (Multiple Listing Service)**
- Most competitive channel
- Retail pricing (usually)
- Opportunity: Days on market 45+, price reductions, failed pending
- Strategy: Build agent relationships, get pocket listings

**Source 2: Wholesalers**
- Pre-negotiated deals
- Wholesale fee adds 5-10% to price
- Opportunity: Speed, less work on your end
- Strategy: Verify ALL numbers independently—never trust provided ARVs

**Source 3: Direct Mail**
- Absentee owners, tax delinquent, pre-foreclosure
- Low response rate (0.5-2%)
- Opportunity: Motivated sellers, less competition
- Strategy: Consistency over intensity—mail monthly for 6+ months

**Source 4: Foreclosures/Auctions**
- Distressed properties
- Cash required (usually)
- Opportunity: Below-market pricing
- Strategy: Know redemption rights, inspect what you can, have title searched

**Source 5: Networking (Toby's ASCEND Community)**
- Referrals from other investors
- Off-market deals
- Opportunity: Relationships create deal flow
- Strategy: Provide value first—give referrals to get referrals

## PART 5: RISK MITIGATION & EXIT STRATEGIES

Hope is not a strategy. Every deal needs a planned exit—preferably two or three.

### The Five Biggest Real Estate Risks

**Risk 1: Overestimating ARV**
- **Cause:** Emotional attachment, cherry-picking comps
- **Mitigation:** Conservative comp analysis, use lowest comp as anchor, get professional BPO

**Risk 2: Underestimating Rehab**
- **Cause:** Photo-only estimates, inexperience, optimism
- **Mitigation:** Contractor walkthrough before purchase, 20-30% contingency, detailed SOW

**Risk 3: Bad Market Timing**
- **Cause:** Buying in declining market, extended hold time
- **Mitigation:** Buy for cashflow (not appreciation), minimize hold time, stay liquid

**Risk 4: Financing Falls Through**
- **Cause:** Lender issues, appraisal problems, rate changes
- **Mitigation:** Pre-approval, backup lender, financing contingency in contracts

**Risk 5: Can't Sell/Rent Quickly**
- **Cause:** Overpriced, wrong market, poor renovation
- **Mitigation:** Price competitively from day one, know rental backup numbers

---

### The Three Exit Strategies

**Exit 1: Fix-and-Flip (6-12 months)**
- Buy, rehab, sell for profit
- Risk: Market turns before sale
- Backup: Refinance and hold as rental

**Exit 2: Buy-and-Hold Rental (5-30 years)**
- Buy, rehab to rental standard, rent for cashflow
- Risk: Bad tenants, maintenance costs, negative cashflow
- Backup: Sell if cashflow doesn't work

**Exit 3: Wholesale (0-30 days)**
- Contract property, assign contract to another investor
- Risk: Can't find buyer
- Backup: Close yourself (if numbers work) or terminate contract

**Toby's Rule:** "Know your exit BEFORE you make an offer. If you don't know how you're getting out, don't get in. And always have Plan B. The deal that only works one way is the deal that will break you."

### Contract Protections

**Inspection Contingency (Essential):**
- Standard 10-14 days for due diligence
- Exit right: Terminate for any reason, earnest money returned
- Use the FULL period—don't waive for competitive advantage unless margin is exceptional

**Financing Contingency (When Using Loans):**
- Standard 21-30 days to secure financing
- Exit right: Terminate if financing denied
- Keep even with hard money lined up—things change

**Title Contingency (Non-Negotiable):**
- Requires clean, marketable title
- Exit right: Terminate if title defects uncurable
- NEVER waive title contingency. ALWAYS get title insurance.

**Assignment Clause (For Flexibility):**
- "Buyer may assign this contract to third party"
- Allows wholesaling as backup exit
- Not all sellers will accept—know your negotiating position

### The 2008 Lesson

"In 2008, I watched investors with 20+ properties lose everything. Here's what they had in common:
- **Over-leveraged:** 100% financing, no equity cushion
- **No cashflow:** Counting on appreciation to bail them out
- **No reserves:** First missed payment triggered cascade
- **Single exit strategy:** Could only sell—and nobody was buying

The investors who survived—and thrived—had:
- **Equity position:** 20%+ equity in each property
- **Positive cashflow:** Properties paid for themselves
- **Cash reserves:** 6+ months of holding costs per property
- **Multiple exits:** Could sell, rent, or refinance

The 2008 crash didn't create winners and losers. It revealed who was building on solid foundation and who was building on sand.
